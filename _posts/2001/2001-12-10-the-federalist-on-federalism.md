## **The Federalist Papers** on Preserving the Federal Balance
In this graduate paper, I analyze federalism as understood by the authors of **The Federalist Papers**. In particular, I challenge the assertion made by scholars like George W. Carey, who argue that Publius’s solution for resolving federal boundary disputes is self-contradictory. Where Carey sees a conflict between Publius’s reliance on the Supreme Court in Federalist No. 39 and his reliance on the people in Federalist Nos. 32 and 46, I argue that Publius actually presents a highly consistent, dual-layered framework. The key to resolving this academic misunderstanding lies in recognizing Publius's vital distinction between an "illegal usurpation" of authority and a "legal exercise" of constitutional power.

## Why This Matters
Understanding this nuanced framework is crucial because it accurately maps the trajectory of American history, explaining how federal power was able to legitimately expand over the last century as it gained the public's trust. Furthermore, as the modern national government increasingly tests its constitutional limits, this analysis serves as a vital reminder that we cannot rely solely on the Supreme Court to preserve our system; the public must remain actively engaged as the ultimate guardians of the constitutional equilibrium.
